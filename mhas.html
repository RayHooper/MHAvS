Martlesham Heath Aviation Society (MHAS) was setup in 1982 to record the history of this famous airfield where many top pilots and aircrew were stationed.

The WW2 Control Tower museum, opened in 2000 and tells the story of this airfield as first a RFC and then an RAF Station. The museum is open every Sunday between the hours of 2:00 to 5:00pm April to October inclusive.  An annual “Open Day” (above right) is held at the Control Tower. Talks on Aviation subjects are presented at Martlesham Community Centre on the first Friday of most months. Outings to aviation related sites also take place.

Martlesham Heath Aviation Society

Control Tower Museum

Martlesham Heath Aviation Society Control Tower Museum contains a large number of artifacts relating to the Royal Flying Corps in 1917 though to WW2 and the subsequent Jet Age. There is an American room which displays material from the WW2 presence of the 356th Fighter Group.

The Control Tower Building was erected in 1943 by the USAAF while they were stationed here.


Barrack Square Memorial

There are two memorials, located at Barrack Square, to remember those who served and died at Martlesham Heath. The left hand pillar commemorates the 72 named airmen from USAAF 356th Fighter Group who served and died during WW2. Dedicated on 13th June 1946, it was the first US memorial raised in Britain after WWII. It was funded from Public Donations.

The memorial on the right commemorates the RAF, Commonwealth and Dominion air forces and allied personnel who served and died at Martlesham Heath during both world wars. It was dedicated in 1991.

The small centre pillar commemorates the work carried out at Martlesham Heath by individual technical aviation units that helped develop aviation technology so that we today can travel safely and in comfort. It was dedicated in 2009.

Church Window & Roll of Honour

At the far end of the St. Michael and All Angels Church is a high window with a modern stained glass panel. The panel commemorates the four periods of the airfield.

In the cabinet beneath is the 356th 'Roll of Honour'.  Seventy two pages, one page for each of 356th personnel who lost their lives while flying from here in World War Two.
