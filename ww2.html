Douglas Bader

Group Captain Douglas Bader first visited Martlesham Heath on May 28th 1940 as a Flight Commander in No.222 Squadron. Later he was the Squadron Leader of No. 242 Squadron based at Martlesham Heath from December 1940 to April 1941, although he left on March 28th 1941.

Squadron Leader Bob Stanford-Tuck was at Martlesham Heath from September 1940 flying Hawker Hurricanes in No. 257 Squadron. He commanded the Squadron in repelling the Italian Air Raid.

Major General Donald Strait (USAAF) was assigned to the 361st Fighter Squadron of the 356th Fighter Group stationed at Martlesham Heath. He piloted the P-47 and P-51 fighters. He was the Top Ace of the Group, having downed 13 1/2 German aircraft during 122 combat missions.

Italian Air Raid

On the 11th November 1940 the Italian Air Force ordered an attack on Harwich Docks.  A fleet of BR20 bombers supported by Fiat CR42 fighters were intercepted by planes from Martlesham commanded by Bob Stanford-Tuck.  One of the bombers crashed in Tangham Forest and the clock from that plane is on show in the MHAS Control Tower Museum.  Chianti wine and cheese were recovered from the plane and ended up on the officers mess table.

The Fiat CR42 seen here crashed on Orfordness and is now on show in RAF Hendon in the same colours as when it crashed.   

Eagle Squadron

In late 1939/early 1940, Britain was suffering terrible casualties in the fight against the Germans and, with a pressing need for pilots and equipment, the government let it be known that they would welcome foreign pilots to join the RAF. In the case of US pilots, the call was picked up by a wealthy American businessman who lived in London, Charles Sweeny, who had ties to the British, Canadian and US governments.

 

The 71st Eagle Squadron was the first of 3 comprising American pilots who flew with the RAF.  It was formed in September of 1940. In April of 1941, the 71st Eagle Squadron moved to Martlesham Heath. At the outset, the pilots were utilized to provide protection to shipping convoys. Eventually, they were sent into battle and acquitted themselves very well. Several of the pilots were awarded Distinguished Flying Crosses by King George VI.

An Eagle Squadron Hurricane flown from Martlesham Heath is being restored at the Brooklands Museum (right courtesy of Brooklands Museum).

Eagle Way on Martlesham Heath commemorates the Eagle Squadron.

USAAF 356th Fighter Group

In 1943 the 356th Fighter Group moved into RAF Martlesham Heath to share the airfield with the RAF.  There were three Squadrons equipped with P-47 Thunderbolts (top right).  These were later replaced with the P-51 Mustang (bottom right).  They lost 72 pilots whose names are recorded in the 356th FG Roll of Honour that is on display in the church of St. Michael & All Angels.  Their names are also on the War Memorial on Barrack Square dedicated in June 1946.
