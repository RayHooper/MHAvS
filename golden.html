The Supermarine S6B (S1596) (shown left) is considered the forerunner of the Spitfire.  This was a 'Float Plane' built to take part in the Schneider Trophy race held every two years.  The Spitfires were piloted by Martlesham Heath personnel. In 1927 by Flt. Lt. Sidney Webster, captain of Ipswich Town Football Club at the time, won the race.  In 1929 Flt. Lt. Waghorn was the winner and in 1931 Flt. Lt. Boothman was the winner.  

On the 26th May 1936 K5054  was at Martlesham Heath for its official trials. The Air Ministry were so impressed that on 3rd June 1936 they issued a contract for 310 Spitfires costing £1.25 million. The prototype achieved a maximum speed of 349 mph. The official report (see above) concluded that: "In general the handling of this aeroplane is such that it can be flown by the average fully trained service fighter pilot".

The Hurricane was one of the most famous of the Hawker aircraft. It accounted for over 60% of the air victories during the Battle of Britain.

The prototype was delivered to Martlesham Heath for tests in early 1936.

During successful trials in March 1936 Sammy Wroath, RAF Test Pilot at the Aeroplane & Armaments Experimental Establishment at Martlesham Heath said, "The aircraft is simple an easy to fly and has no apparent vices". RAF acceptance was granted in June of that year.

A Royal visit to Martlesham Heath took place on the 8th July 1936.  King Edward had in January of that year become King following the death of his father George V. The party all wore black armbands as a mark of respect for the deceased King. Edward VIII later abdicated and was never crowned.

All the latest prototype aeroplanes were lined up for inspection. They were there undergoing flight testing etc. at Martlesham Heath.

Also in the line up was K5083, the Hawker Hurricane.  The Hurricane  was informally christened by Edward during the visit (left).

The prototype Supermarine Spitfire receiving very close attention by King Edward VIII and his brother.  Just by the propeller is J R Mitchell the designer of the plane.

RAF Martlesham Heath worked closely with RAF Bawdsey on the development of Radio Directional Finding (RDF) later called Radar. The picture shows an Avro Anson that Keith Wood (right) and Eddie (Taffy) Bowen used in September 1937 to track the Home Fleet on manoeuvres in the North Sea.  It took off from Martlesham Heath piloted by Sgt. Pilot Snaith and found the fleet by radar.  They then noticed on the oscilloscope a group of small blips.  The Radar had detected the planes coming up from the aircraft carrier, HMS Courageous. They had been sent up to see who was flying over the fleet.  It was the first time that an 'Air to Air' radar contact was seen. Radar was taken very seriously indeed from then on (D Flight MH personnel - above left).
