A Porte Baby Flying boat pictured at the Naval Air station Felixstowe with a Bristol Scout atop with 'wheels'.  The Porte Baby was designed and flown by the station commander John Cyril Porte.  The Bristol Scout was  instrumental in Martlesham Heath becoming a Royal Flying Corps airfield. The Porte Baby being a flying boat took off on the River Orwell/Stour Estuary.  At a predetermined height the Bristol Scout and its pilot were released and then headed for Martlesham Heath in order to land.

In 1916 the Aeroplane Experimental Flight was moved from Upavon in Wiltshire to Martlesham Heath. Henry Tizard and his committee was set up to find a new airfield close to Orfordness where the 'Armament Experimental Flight' had moved in 1915. Martlesham Heath was close to a railway station (Little Bealings) for access to and from London. Tizard had seen where the Bristol Scout had landed and decided that Martlesham Heath was to be the location for the development of a new airfield for the Royal Flying Corps (RFC).

The station was dedicated on the 17th January 1917 and on the right is a picture of the initial staff.  The RFC being in its infancy was made up of personnel from Army and Naval units.  The achievements of each of these men during their service careers was significant.

Royal Flying Corps

The pioneer aerial photographer, Walden Hammond, started at Farnborough and then went to Orfordness. He set up the Photographic Flight at Martlesham Heath in 1917 (centre). His  initially hand-held camera was large, constructed of wood and used glass plates. Whilst in flight he was not strapped in and did not have a parachute.


He developed an idea for taking aerial reconnaissance shots at an oblique angle. He attached a perspective grid to indicate range (left). The Army required that pictures were taken from vertically overhead, an extremely dangerous procedure. His idea was not officially adopted for military purposes until 1941. Today, they are known as “Air Perspective Maps”. He received no recognition.


He photographed the surrender of the German U boat fleet at Harwich at the end of WW1 (right).

The first parachute unit was formed at MH in April 1924.  Flt. Lt. John Potter headed a team affectionately known as 'The Loonies'.  Potter later  developed the 'Potter Parachute'.  The picture shows the parachute attached to the pilot/aircrew's back with a static line which was then attached to the plane and which pulls open the parachute as he leaves the plane.

Parachute Unit

Martlesham Heath was responsible for the testing of all types of aeroplanes. Fighters, Bombers like the Bristol Braemar II (above).

The Beardmore Inflexible (above) was the largest aeroplane to come to Martlesham Heath. It had a wingspan of 160ft and wheels measuring 8ft in diameter.  Built by the Beardmore company on the Clyde it was shipped to Ipswich docks and brought by road to MH and assembled.  It was the first all metal plane and not designed for anything other than a flying test bed.  It rarely left MH and only then as a flying example of a transport plane.

To give you some idea of the Beardmore Inflexible’s size, the picture shows its pilot Flt. Lt. Jack Noakes.
