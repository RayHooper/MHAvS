Blind Landing

Research was undertaken post WW2 to make military and civil aircraft safe in poor visibility. The Blind Landing Experimental Unit (BLEU) was formed at Woodbridge on 1 October 1945 and moved to Martlesham Heath in July 1946, but continued to use the Woodbridge airfield. BLEU later moved to Bedford.

The target was to develop an automatic landing system for zero visibility.

The system relied on Radio beams with the signals fed into the automatic pilot in the aircraft.

A number of different aircraft types were used including the Lancaster, Canberra, Devon, Dakota, Viking, Meteor, Varsity, An and Oxford. The development of accurate altimeters was essential for measuring the height near the ground.

The first fully automatic landing in revenue service was made in Trident G-ARPR which landed at Heathrow from Paris on 10 June 1965.

Battle of Britain Flight

In May 1958  the Battle of Britain Flight was based at North Weald but a new site was needed. It was decided that the Flight should move to Martlesham Heath. Each Year the Battle of Britain Flypast over London was led by a single Hurricane and a single Spitfire from the Flight.  

In November 1961 , because of the impending closure of Martlesham Heath airfield by the RAF, a further move had to be made and the Flight went to Horsham St Faith.  It had been depleted and only consisted of the Hurricane LF363 and Spitfire XIX , PM631 when it moved.

Airfield Closure

RAF Martlesham Heath closed on the 25th April 1963 so bringing to an end 46 years of aircraft and avionics activity here.

Above left are the 'A' Flight & 'B' Flight hangers.  'A' Flight housed the fighter aircraft that came to be tested.  'B' Flight housed the bombers, sadly now these are only memories.  These were pulled down and in their place the Tesco 24 hour store was built.  

Last Flying Day at Martlesham Heath

After the RAF station closed,  the airfield was used by one and two of the firms for their own private aircraft. It was also used by a parachute school and also other private aircraft. On one occasion Peter Scott, the ornithologist, who landed here and immediately left his glider and went to look at a nest he had seen as he arrived.

The runways were also used by the locals to practice driving.

The very last flying occasion was in March 1979 when a final fly-out was arranged which was attended by the late Gordon Kinsey, then President of MHAS and author of a book about RAF Martlesham Heath.This attracted a good crowd to see the 35 aircraft that visited.
